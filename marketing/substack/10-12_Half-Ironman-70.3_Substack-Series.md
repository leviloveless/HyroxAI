## POST 1 — For sport scientists & coaches

### 70.3 Is Where the Finishing-vs-Competing Gap Opens Up

**At ~3:45–5:30, the half is decided by sub-threshold aerobic capacity and durability. A 10-hour week finishes it. Competing in the back half is a different physiological ask.**

The 70.3 sits at an inflection point in the volume–intensity argument. Olympic distance still lets intensity substitute well for volume; Ironman decisively does not. The half is where the substitution begins to break, and it breaks in a specific place: the back half of the run.

Duration is the reason. A ~4–5.5 hour race is long enough that the volume-gated adaptations start to bind — fat-oxidation capacity, capillary density, GI tolerance for sustained fueling, and connective-tissue robustness in the run. These are the Part IV traits that *cannot* be bought with intensity at any price, because they accumulate through low-intensity volume rather than through near-maximal work. Below the event's volume demand, the athlete progressively sacrifices them in rough proportion to how far short they fall.

The practical translation is the distinction every 70.3 coach should make explicit to the athlete:

- **A 10-hour week produces a credible mid-pack finisher.** Split near swim 20% / bike 47% / run 28% / strength 5%, distribution ~68/17/15, anchored by a weekly long ride (2.5–3 h) and long run (75–90 min), two quality sessions, and 2–3 swims. What it sacrifices is late-race durability depth — the difference between holding half-marathon pace and fading in the final 5–7 km.
- **A 20-hour week optimizes durability, fat oxidation, GI tolerance, and genuine competitiveness**, at a more polarized 80/8/12, with a big Z1 base, a long brick with race-nutrition rehearsal, and only two quality sessions. It sacrifices very little for the distance.

Between those two budgets lies most of the field, and the coaching judgment is about honesty: a time-limited athlete can be well-prepared to *finish* strong on 10 hours, but *competing* — defending pace when the race is actually decided — increasingly requires the volume they don't have. Name that trade rather than papering over it with more intensity, which at this distance mostly raises cost without buying durability.

The strength allocation stays small (~5%) and serves economy and run durability, kept low enough to avoid competing with the aerobic quality work for recovery — the same concurrent-training discipline that governs every event in this series.

## POST 2 — For competitive athletes

### For a 70.3, Be Honest About Finishing vs. Competing

**Ten hours a week gets you across the line in good shape. Racing the back half of the run is a different — and more volume-hungry — goal.**

The half-Ironman is where a lot of athletes get quietly humbled. Not in the swim, not on the bike, not even at the start of the run — but in the last 5 to 7 kilometers, where the race is actually decided. Whether you hold your pace there or fall apart comes down to *durability*, and durability is built with volume you can't fake with intensity.

So the first question isn't "what's my plan?" It's "am I training to finish strong, or to compete?"

**Training to finish strong (a credible mid-pack race) — ~10 hours/week:**

- Split around swim 20% / bike 47% / run 28% / strength 5%; intensity ~68/17/15.
- Anchors: a weekly long ride (2.5–3 hours), a long run (75–90 minutes), two quality sessions, and 2–3 swims.
- This gets you fit, gets you to the line, and lets you run — but the deep back-half durability isn't fully there. Respect the final 5k.

**Training to compete — closer to 20 hours/week:**

- More easy volume, a more polarized ~80/8/12, a long brick with full race-nutrition rehearsal.
- This is where fat oxidation, gut tolerance, and run durability actually come in — the stuff that keeps your pace honest when it hurts.

Two hard-won lessons regardless of budget. First, **rehearse your fueling on the long brick, not on race day** — GI blow-ups end more 70.3s than fitness does. Second, **don't convert a short week into all-hard training** to "make up" for missing hours. It raises fatigue without buying the durability the distance demands. Keep the majority easy even when time is tight.

Pick your honest goal, train the matching budget, and the race stops surprising you.

## POST 3 — For everyday readers

### The Half-Ironman: What "Finishing Well" Actually Takes

**A 1.9k swim, 90k bike, and 21k run. Big — but with the right plan, very finishable. Here's what to aim for.**

A 70.3 (also called a half-Ironman) is a serious day: a nearly-2k swim, a 90k bike, and a half-marathon to finish, usually taking four to five and a half hours. It's a bucket-list race for a lot of people, and the good news is you don't need to be a pro to finish it well — you need consistency and a couple of key long sessions each week.

Here's the one thing to understand about this distance: **the race gets hard at the end of the run.** The swim and bike feel manageable; the first few kilometers of the run feel fine. Then the last stretch of the run is where people struggle. The way you prepare for that isn't by training harder — it's by gradually building up your easy endurance so your body can keep going when it's tired.

What that looks like in practice, on roughly 10 hours a week:

- **One long ride and one long run every week.** These are the backbone — build them up slowly over the months. A ride around 2.5–3 hours and a run around 75–90 minutes.
- **Keep most of your training easy.** Comfortable, conversational effort. Save hard efforts for one or two sessions a week.
- **Practice eating and drinking on your long sessions.** More half-Ironmans fall apart because of an upset stomach than because of fitness. Figure out your food and drink in training, not on race day.

Ten hours a week, done consistently over a training block, gets a regular person to a strong finish. You may not be racing for a podium — that takes more time than most of us have — but finishing a 70.3 feeling good is a completely realistic goal.

Respect the distance, build your long sessions patiently, keep most of it easy, and practice your fueling. That's the whole recipe.

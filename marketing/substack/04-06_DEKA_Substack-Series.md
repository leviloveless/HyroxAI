## POST 1 — For sport scientists & coaches

### DEKA Is Three Different Events. Program It That Way.

**No lab study of DEKA exists yet. But format and duration tell you almost everything you need about the intensity–volume trade — if you stop treating "DEKA" as one thing.**

DEKA is ten functional zones performed in three formats, and the formats span the widest internal range of any event in the hybrid-endurance space:

- **DEKA STRONG** — ten zones back-to-back, no running, ~10–14 minutes: a glycolytic/anaerobic-power sprint.
- **DEKA MILE** — zones plus a 160 m run before each (~1 mile total), ~16–22 minutes: mixed power-endurance.
- **DEKA FIT** — zones plus a 500 m run before each (~5 km total), ~28–37 minutes: glycolytic-oxidative, the closest analogue to HYROX but about 40% shorter and therefore run at a higher relative intensity.

There is no peer-reviewed physiological study of DEKA — a genuine evidence gap — so these profiles are inferred from format and duration rather than measured. But the programming consequence is robust regardless, because it follows from the report's central duration thesis: **the shorter and more glycolytic the event, the more intensity substitutes for volume, and the more a strength/power emphasis pays.**

That places STRONG at one extreme. Of every event in this analysis, DEKA STRONG is the most intensity-substitutable — a ~10–14 minute effort is dominated by glycolytic power and strength-endurance, adaptations that respond to near-maximal work far more than to accumulated aerobic volume. FIT sits at the other end, approaching HYROX programming: still glycolytic, but long enough that aerobic contribution and a degree of durability start to matter, at a higher-intensity, lower-total-volume setting than HYROX because it's shorter.

**What this means for distribution and volume.** For FIT at a 10-hour week, a distribution near 60/18/22 (Z1/Z2/Z3) with 2–3 strength/power sessions fits the glycolytic-oxidative demand; move toward STRONG and you shift roughly 10 points of Z1 into Z3 and add a strength/power session at every budget. The single most useful ceiling claim in the DEKA section: **more than about 20 hours a week has no event-specific justification for any DEKA format.** The event demand is simply capped below the point where big aerobic volume pays off. A coach prescribing 25-hour weeks for a DEKA STRONG athlete is training for an event that doesn't exist.

**The concurrent-training logic still applies, inverted from HYROX.** Because STRONG and MILE lean harder on glycolytic power and strength-endurance, they justify more strength volume than HYROX does — but the same protection principle holds: keep the explosive-power work high-quality and low-volume, and don't let a large aerobic block erode rate-of-force-development (the concurrent-training–vulnerable adaptation, SMD ≈ −0.28) that these formats actively reward.

**Caveat:** with no direct physiology, DEKA prescriptions lean on transferable evidence and coaching judgment more than the other events here. Label them as such to athletes. The direction is well-founded; the exact numbers are best-estimate.

## POST 2 — For competitive athletes

### The DEKA Format You Picked Should Change Your Entire Training Plan

**STRONG, MILE, and FIT are not the same race with different lengths. Train them the same way and you'll leave a lot on the floor.**

Most people training for DEKA pick a format and then train "for DEKA" generically. That's a mistake, because the three formats ask for genuinely different things.

**DEKA STRONG (~10–14 min, no running)** is a power sprint. It's the most "trainable on low hours" event in the entire hybrid-endurance space — a short, glycolytic grind where being strong and powerful matters more than aerobic base. If STRONG is your event, **5 focused hours a week is a complete, competitive program.** Bias toward the harder end: more Zone 3 work, an extra strength/power session, and race-pace circuits through the ten zones. You do not need long easy volume to be good at a 12-minute event.

**DEKA FIT (~28–37 min, ~5k of running)** is the closest thing to a HYROX. It's long enough that your aerobic engine and your ability to hold power while tired both matter. At a 10-hour week, think roughly: run ~40%, zone circuits ~32%, strength/power ~28%; intensity around 60/18/22; anchors of two zone circuits, a VO₂ session, a threshold run, a long aerobic session, and 2–3 strength/power days.

**DEKA MILE** sits right between them — split the difference.

**The prescription that matters most:** for FIT, a well-built 10-hour week makes you a genuinely competitive age-grouper. For STRONG and MILE, 10 hours is already elite-level preparation. And across every format, **going past ~20 hours a week buys you almost nothing** — the events just aren't long enough to reward big aerobic volume. If you're grinding 20+ hour weeks for DEKA, you're training for an event that doesn't exist.

Pick your format first. Then train the specific demand: sharp and powerful for STRONG, balanced power-endurance for FIT.

## POST 3 — For everyday readers

### Thinking About DEKA? Pick the Right Version First.

**DEKA comes in three flavors, and choosing the right one for you makes training way simpler — and shorter — than you'd expect.**

DEKA is ten "zones" — stations like lunges, rowing, ski erg, farmers carries, and burpees — and it comes in three versions. Knowing which one you're doing changes everything about how you train.

- **DEKA STRONG** is all ten zones back-to-back, no running, done in about 10–14 minutes. It's short, intense, and brutal in a sprint-y way.
- **DEKA MILE** adds a short run before each zone — about a mile total.
- **DEKA FIT** adds longer runs before each zone — about 5k total — and takes half an hour or so.

Here's the good news, especially if you're time-crunched: **the shorter the version, the less you have to train.** DEKA STRONG is basically a 12-minute power test. You can be genuinely competitive at it on about **5 hours a week** — a couple of hard circuits through the zones, two short strength sessions, and one run. No marathon-style mileage required.

If you pick FIT (the longer one with more running), you'll want a bit more — some easy running to build your engine, plus the zone work and strength. But even then, you don't need to live in the gym. Around 10 hours a week is plenty for most people, and honestly, going much beyond that is overkill for DEKA no matter which version you do.

So the simplest advice: **choose your format based on your time and what sounds fun.** Short on hours or love the all-out grind? STRONG. Want more of an endurance challenge? FIT. Then train the thing you signed up for — power and intensity for the short ones, a bit more easy running for the longer one.
